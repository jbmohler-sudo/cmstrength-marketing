What Is a Macrocycle?

A macrocycle is the highest-level organizational unit in periodized training. It's a long-term progression plan spanning multiple weeks, organized into distinct training blocks, each with a specific physiological goal.

CMS employs a 4-block macrocycle structure:

  • Skill Block (Weeks 1–4) — Work capacity, movement quality, technique refinement
  • Strength Block (Weeks 5–8) — Increased volume + load, neural adaptations
  • Peak Block (Weeks 9–12) — Intensity emphasis, competition-specific simulation
  • Test Week (Week 13) — Meet attempt or max effort test

Each block is 4 weeks long (except Test Week, which is 1 week), and week 4 of each block serves as a deload week to allow recovery and prevent overtraining.

The Block Structure

Deload Weeks (Weeks 4, 8, 12)

Deload weeks automatically reduce volume and intensity across all lifts. RPE targets drop by 1–1.5 points, and sets/reps for accessory work are halved. This allows the nervous system and joints to recover while maintaining training stimulus.

Why Deload? Without planned recovery, cumulative fatigue leads to stagnation or injury. CMS automates this process—no user guesswork required.

The 13-Week Progression: Bench Specialization Example

Week 1–4: Skill Block

Goal: Build movement quality and work capacity.

  • Secondary lift: Larsen Press (or weak-point specific alternative)
  • RPE targets: 6–7 on top sets
  • Volume emphasis: 3–4 sets per lift
  • Deload Week (Week 4): RPE 5–6, reduced volume

Week 5–8: Strength Block

Goal: Increase load and neural adaptations.

  • Secondary lift: Competition Bench Variation (Close Grip, Paused)
  • RPE targets: 7–8 on top sets
  • Volume emphasis: 4–5 sets per lift
  • Deload Week (Week 8): RPE 6–7, reduced volume

Week 9–12: Peak Block

Goal: Maximize intensity and competition readiness.

  • Secondary lift: Heavy specialty press (board press, pin bench)
  • RPE targets: 8–9 on top sets
  • Volume emphasis: 3–4 sets per lift (lower, heavier)
  • Deload Week (Week 12): RPE 7–8, minimal volume

Week 13: Test Week

Goal: Compete or test 1RM.

  • 3 attempts at competition lift (Opener, Second, Final)
  • No prescribed weight—lifter chooses attempts
  • Successful meet prep or max-effort testing
Key Insight: The 4-week block length is not arbitrary. Research shows that 4 weeks is optimal for neural adaptation in strength athletes. Shorter blocks don't allow sufficient adaptation; longer blocks risk accumulated fatigue without strategic deload.

Intelligent Block Transitions

When a lifter completes Block 1 (Skill), the system doesn't just move to Block 2—it applies a block gate. The gate asks:

  • Has the lifter completed all sessions in the block?
  • Is readiness adequate to advance?
  • Are there any joint health flags that suggest modifying the weak point?

If the answer to any question is "no," the lifter may repeat or modify the block. This prevents jamming into a new block unprepared.

Weak Point Re-Assessment: At the end of each block (after Week 4, 8, 12), the lifter can optionally reassess their weak point. This allows CMS to adapt secondary lift selection based on recent performance data.

Post-Cycle Management

After Test Week, CMS offers two paths:

  • Restart (Recommended) — Begin a new 13-week cycle with updated 1RM baseline
  • Deload Week Extension — Take 1–2 extra recovery weeks before the next cycle

The lifter's e1RM (estimated 1RM) is automatically recalculated based on all session data from the completed cycle. This becomes the new baseline for the next cycle's load calculations.

Why This Matters

A well-structured macrocycle prevents burnout, manages fatigue intelligently, and ensures that every training session serves a specific physiological purpose. CMS automates the periodization logic so you can focus on execution.
